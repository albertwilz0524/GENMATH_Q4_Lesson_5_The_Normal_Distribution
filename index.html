Properties of the Normal Probability Distribution

The normal probability distribution has several key defining characteristics:

1. Bell-Shaped

The distribution curve forms a smooth, continuous bell shape, meaning data is concentrated near the middle.

2. Symmetrical

The curve is perfectly symmetrical about its center. If you fold it in half, the left and right sides match exactly.

3. Mean = Median = Mode

The mean ($\mu$), the median, and the mode all coincide at the exact center of the distribution.

4. Width depends on Standard Deviation

The standard deviation ($\sigma$) determines how spread out the curve is. A larger $\sigma$ makes the curve wider and flatter; a smaller $\sigma$ makes it taller and narrower.

Properties of the Normal Probability Distribution

5. Asymptotic Tails

The tails of the curve flatten out indefinitely along the horizontal axis. They always approach the axis but never touch it. This is known as being asymptotic to the base line.

6. Total Area = 1 (or 100%)

The entire area under the curve is exactly 1. Thus, the area represents the probability, proportion, or percentage associated with specific sets of measurement values.

The Empirical Rule (68-95-99.7 Rule)

For any normal distribution, the data is divided into predictable regions based on the standard deviation ($\sigma$):

  • 68% of the population falls within 1 standard deviation from the mean ($\mu \pm 1\sigma$).
  • 95% of the population falls within 2 standard deviations from the mean ($\mu \pm 2\sigma$).
  • 99.7% of the population falls within 3 standard deviations from the mean ($\mu \pm 3\sigma$).

Guided Example 1: Adult Women Heights

A company is conducting a study on the heights of adult women in a certain city. The heights are normally distributed with a mean of 160 cm ($\mu = 160$) and a standard deviation of 10 cm ($\sigma = 10$).

What percentage of the adult women:

1. Are taller than 160 cm?
Since 160 is the mean, exactly half the curve is above it. 50%.
2. Have heights between 140 cm and 180 cm?
140 cm is $-2\sigma$ ($160-20$) and 180 cm is $+2\sigma$ ($160+20$). By the empirical rule, this covers exactly 95%.
3. Have exactly 160.0000... cm in height?
0%. Height is a Continuous Random Variable. The probability of picking one exact, infinitely precise mathematical point is 0 (the area of a line with zero width is 0). We can only calculate percentages for ranges.
4. Are taller than 180 cm?
180 cm is $+2\sigma$. The middle area is 95%. The area outside is $100\% - 95\% = 5\%$. Since we only want the right side (taller), we divide by 2 to get 2.5%.
5. Have heights between 150 cm and 170 cm?
150 cm is $-1\sigma$ and 170 cm is $+1\sigma$. By the empirical rule, this covers exactly 68%.

Try This! Adult Women Heights

Using the same data: Mean ($\mu$) = 160 cm, and Standard Deviation ($\sigma$) = 10 cm.

Calculate the percentage of adult women who:

  1. Are shorter than 170 cm?
  2. Have heights between 150 cm and 190 cm?
  3. Have exactly 175.000... cm in height?
  4. Are taller than 190 cm?
  5. Have heights between 130 cm and 160 cm?

1. Shorter than 170 cm: 84%
Reasoning: 170 cm is $+1\sigma$. Area to the left of the mean is 50%. Area from mean to $+1\sigma$ is 34%. Total = $50\% + 34\% = 84\%$.

2. Between 150 cm and 190 cm: 83.85%
Reasoning: 150 is $-1\sigma$ (34%). 190 is $+3\sigma$ (49.85%, which is half of 99.7%). Total = $34\% + 49.85\% = 83.85\%$.

3. Exactly 175.000... cm: 0%
Reasoning: Continuous variable. The probability of an exact point is always 0.

4. Taller than 190 cm: 0.15%
Reasoning: 190 is $+3\sigma$. The area within $\pm 3\sigma$ is 99.7%. Area outside is 0.3%. We want only the right tail, so $0.3\% \div 2 = 0.15\%$.

5. Between 130 cm and 160 cm: 49.85%
Reasoning: 130 is $-3\sigma$ and 160 is the mean. This is exactly half of the $\pm 3\sigma$ region (99.7% / 2).

Guided Example 2: Anime Watching

The amount of time that Sagradan students spend watching anime per day is normally distributed with a mean of 55 minutes ($\mu = 55$) and a standard deviation of 15 minutes ($\sigma = 15$).

1. What percentage watch anime for more than 70 minutes?
70 mins is exactly $+1\sigma$. The right half is 50%. The region from mean to $+1\sigma$ is 34%. The area beyond $+1\sigma$ is $50\% - 34\% = $ 16%.
2. What percentage watch anime for less than 40 minutes?
40 mins is exactly $-1\sigma$. Similar to Q1, the area to the left of $-1\sigma$ is $50\% - 34\% = $ 16%.
3. What percentage watch anime between 25 and 85 minutes?
25 mins is $-2\sigma$ ($55 - 30$). 85 mins is $+2\sigma$ ($55 + 30$). The Empirical Rule states this covers 95% of the data.
4. What percentage watch exactly 55.000... minutes?
0%. Time is continuous. There is zero chance of hitting a perfectly exact decimal measurement with infinite precision.
5. If 500 students are surveyed, how many watch for more than 70 minutes?
From Q1, the probability is 16%. We find 16% of 500: $0.16 \times 500 = $ 80 students.

Try This! Glancing at Your Crush

The average amount of time Sagradan students spend glancing at their crush is normally distributed, with a mean of 2 seconds ($\mu = 2$) and a standard deviation of 0.5 seconds ($\sigma = 0.5$).

  1. What percentage glance for less than 1 second?
  2. What percentage glance for between 1.5 and 2.5 seconds?
  3. What percentage glance for more than 3.5 seconds?
  4. What percentage glance for exactly 2.000... seconds?
  5. About how many Grade 11 students glance for less than 1 second? (There are 135 Grade 11 students in MDSF).

1. Less than 1 second: 2.5%
Reasoning: 1 sec is $-2\sigma$. Left half is 50%. Mean to $-2\sigma$ is 47.5%. $50\% - 47.5\% = 2.5\%$.

2. Between 1.5 and 2.5 seconds: 68%
Reasoning: 1.5 is $-1\sigma$ and 2.5 is $+1\sigma$. By Empirical Rule, this is 68%.

3. More than 3.5 seconds: 0.15%
Reasoning: 3.5 is $+3\sigma$. The area beyond $+3\sigma$ on the right tail is half of 0.3%, which is 0.15%.

4. Exactly 2.000... seconds: 0%
Reasoning: Time is a continuous variable. The probability of an exact point is 0.

5. How many Grade 11 students (< 1 second)? ~3 students
Reasoning: Find 2.5% of 135: $0.025 \times 135 = 3.375$, which rounds to approximately 3 students.
